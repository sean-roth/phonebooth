# Decision-maker call script — Midwest manufacturers

Companion to `sales-strategy.md`. This is the spoken doctrine for the moment a
decision maker (owner / plant manager / ops) is on the line. For the
manufacturer segment it supersedes the opener in
`docs/branding/cold-call-pitch.md` (contractor-era doctrine — kept as history,
not edited).

Field diagnosis that forced the change: gatekeepers were passing, but the
15-minute ask stalled every time. Two causes, two changes:

1. **The stakes moved INTO the opener.** The old lock ("save the
   senior-guy-pulled-off-the-line pain for discovery") starved the only moment
   that decides whether discovery happens.
2. **The interviews moved INTO the pitch.** "I take your procedures" silently
   disqualifies every owner whose documentation is thin or embarrassing — he
   never says so; he says "we're good" and hangs up. "I sit down with your
   people" deletes that objection before it forms. (The interviews were always
   part of the offer; they were just never in the words.)

## Voice (inherited, non-negotiable)
Plain, short sentences. Say "onboarding" / "module," never "training." Never
say AI, compliance, platform, solution, SCORM, LMS. Peer to peer, craftsman to
craftsman — no thanks-for-your-time, no vendor posture, never apologize for
being new.

## The opener (~15 seconds, spoken)

> "Morning, this is Sean — I build onboarding courses for manufacturers.
> Right now, most of how your floor actually runs is in your people's heads,
> and it clocks out with them at shift change. I sit down with those people —
> and with you — fold in whatever procedures you've got written down, and
> turn it into one course the whole crew can check. Four quick questions
> would tell me if it's a fit: got three minutes now, or is there a better
> time this week?"

Beat by beat:
- **Category first.** He files the caller in five seconds or hangs up.
- **The problem is a threat, not a schedule.** The knowledge clocks out,
  calls in sick, retires, walks across town. One clause of consequence —
  never more.
- **People before paper.** The order tells him the offer works even if the
  binder is a mess (it usually is) without ever saying so. "And with you"
  makes him a source, not a student. "Whatever" carries the no-judgment.
- **The ask offers two yeses.** "Now or later this week" converts better
  than "worth fifteen minutes?", which invites "send me something."

Test before changing a word: read it out loud, standing, with a timer. Over
~20 seconds at call speed, cut from the mechanism sentence — never from the
problem or the ask.

## Two gears

### Gear 1 — three minutes, live, now
If he's talking, take it now. A DM giving three minutes on the spot is worth
more than one who vaguely agrees to a call that has to survive his calendar.
Run Q1-lite and Q3's who-barrel (below), then close by booking the fifteen
with HIS OWN words as the reason:

> "That thing you said about [the changeover checklist] — that's exactly
> what I'd build first. Worth the full fifteen minutes to map it out?"

Gear 1's job is not fit/no-fit. It is to earn the fifteen.

Line note: everything — cold dials and discovery — goes out on the Mint
cellular number, so gear 1 runs on the same audio as the fifteen. No line to
switch. If he's talking, take the minutes.

### Gear 2 — the fifteen minutes, scheduled or granted
All four questions in full. Ends the way
`docs/branding/discovery-questions.md` says: "here's what I think would work
for you, and here's what it costs" — or an honest "I don't think I'm the
right fit, here's why." Both build the position. The people you tell no
remember you and refer.

Discovery opener (the relocated humility line — never use it in the cold
opener): "I don't know your day-to-day — you and your guys do. That's why
this is four questions and not a pitch."

## The four questions — manufacturer wording

Adapted from `docs/branding/discovery-questions.md` (written contractor-era).
Same architecture — narrative, trigger, content, history — same brand
reasoning. Two changes: Q2 rewritten for outbound honesty, Q3 gains the
who-barrel that casts the interview.

**1. Narrative.** "How does a new hire actually get up to speed right now —
after HR's paperwork day, who teaches him the line? Walk me through his
first couple weeks."
Gear-1 lite: "How do new guys get up to speed right now — who handles it?"
Listening for: who owns the first week, what materials exist, where it
breaks, frustrated vs resigned. Frustrated is buying. Resigned isn't yet.

**2. Trigger — outbound-honest version.** "When I called, did this land on
something already on your list, or is it the first time it's come up?"
The old wording ("what's pushing you to look at this right now?") assumed he
came to us. He didn't — we called him. Listening for: a specific trigger
(buying), vague agreement (not yet), or "first time it's come up" (you're
educating — longer cadence, don't force the close).

**3. Content + casting (load-bearing).** "What's the most important thing a
new operator needs to know in his first week — and who on your floor knows
it best? The guy everyone goes to when something's off."
Once he names someone: "If I sat with him for an hour, what would he tell me
that isn't written down anywhere?" Then: "What do new guys typically get
wrong before they learn it?"
This one question scopes the first module, names the interview subject for
the pilot, and makes him picture the mechanism before it's ever proposed.
Write his story down verbatim — it is the seed of module one.

**4. History.** "What have you tried, and what got in the way?"
Unchanged. Tried nothing = earlier than he sounds. Tried cheap and bounced =
good, he knows cheap doesn't work. Tried expensive and failed = learn why
before reproducing it.

## After the four
Fit: "Here's what I think would work for you. The pilot is one module, built
from [the thing he named in Q3], $3,500, delivered in about two weeks." Not
a fit: say so, and say why. Pricing detail and the after-the-yes runbook
live in `docs/` and Drive — this script ends at the yes.

## What did NOT change
The gatekeeper approach — it works; leave it alone. The demo
(sopsnobodyreads.com/demo) as proof. "One of my first customers" as the
reason for the pilot price. One number for everything: the Mint cellular
line is the outreach identity, cold and discovery alike.
