# 06 — Targeting Brief: Phase 1

## Purpose of this document

Specifies who Sean is calling, why, and how. The Engineer doesn't need this to build the system; Sean needs it to fill the leads list with the right businesses on Sunday and to know what to say on Monday. The dashboard reads this conceptually, not literally — but it's checked into the repo as the canonical record of what Phase 1 is about.

## The two-headed goal

Phase 1 isn't only about revenue. It has two aligned goals:

1. **Make money** via small AI/web projects with Chicago small businesses
2. **Discover patterns** — what do these owners repeatedly complain about? What's eating their time? Where's the productizable opportunity hiding?

The pain-points field on every call is the bridge between these two goals. Calls that don't book still produce data.

## Positioning: AI handyman

The pitch is *AI handyman*, not AI specialist. Reasoning:

- Specialization comes *out of* customer discovery, not before it
- The user has both AI skills and web development skills; combining them is unusual and valuable for tech-resistant small businesses
- Small business owners don't buy "AI" — they buy "fixes for things that bother them"

This means:

- Lead with practical problems, not technology
- Quote 1-4 week scoped projects, fixed price
- Offer web work alongside AI work without separating them in the pitch
- Don't try to upsell or cross-sell on the first call

## Ideal customer profile

**Size:** 1-15 employees, owner-operated. The person answering the phone is the person who decides.

**Geography:** Chicago city limits + immediate inner suburbs (Oak Park, Evanston, Berwyn, Cicero). All calls are made from Conifer, Colorado, but the targeting is Chicago because:
- Sean is planning to move to Chicago
- Calling Chicago businesses now builds geographic familiarity for the eventual move
- A Chicago number (312 or 773) on caller ID gives local credibility

**Industries (Phase 1 sprint focus):**

Best initial targets are businesses that:
- Have a website (proves they engage digitally at all)
- Show signs of operational pain that AI/web work could relieve
- Have margins to pay $1-5K for a project
- Are owner-operated (no procurement, no committee)

Strong candidates:
- **Specialty trades:** HVAC, plumbing, electrical, roofing, painting, landscaping
- **Independent retailers** with online presence: specialty shops, vintage/antique, boutiques, food specialty
- **Small professional services:** independent CPAs, small law practices (1-3 attorneys), insurance agents, financial advisors
- **Specialty manufacturing / fabricators:** custom signage, cabinetry, metalwork, food production
- **Wellness/personal services:** independent gyms, dental practices (1-3 dentists), vet clinics, chiropractors

**Anti-signals (skip these):**
- Chains or franchises
- Tech-forward language on website ("AI-powered," "innovative," "digital transformation")
- Recent funding announcements
- Generic agency-style websites
- Real estate agencies (saturated with vendors pitching them)
- Anything that looks like a tech startup

**Opening recommendation for first sprint:** pick TWO industries from the list above and focus the entire week on them. Suggested pair: **specialty trades + independent retailers**. Reasons: high pain density in trades, retailers often need web/e-commerce work which is a natural opener, similar conversational rhythm makes each call sharpen the next.

## The pitch

**The opener (first 8 seconds):**

> "Hi [name], this is Sean Roth — I'm calling out of the blue. I do small AI and web projects for local Chicago businesses, usually one to four weeks, fixed price. I'm calling around because I'm building a Chicago small-business practice and trying to learn what actually eats your time. If there's a fit for a project, great. If not, I'd love to hear what's frustrating you because I'm building tools around real problems. Got 30 seconds?"

Why this works:
- "Calling out of the blue" — names the awkwardness; relieves it
- "Small AI and web projects" — flexible offering, doesn't lock into one thing
- "1-4 weeks, fixed price" — small commitment, not a big consulting engagement
- "Building a Chicago practice" — geographic affinity, shows commitment to the area
- "Trying to learn what eats your time" — they're an expert being asked their opinion, not a target being sold to
- "If there's a fit, great. If not, I'd love to hear..." — gives them permission to refuse, which paradoxically makes them more likely to engage
- "Got 30 seconds?" — permission-based ask; gives them control

## Disqualifiers (when to politely end the call)

If any of these emerge, Sean ends the call respectfully — these aren't fits:

- They have AI/automation vendors they're already happy with
- The problem they describe is too large for 1-4 week scope
- They want a strategic partner, not a project contractor
- They have no specific manual pain, just curiosity about AI
- They need an employee, not a contractor
- They're price-shopping with multiple vendors

Politeness matters because Chicago small business is interconnected. The person you brush off today might be the referral source for someone else next week.

## Goal of each call

**Primary:** Book a 30-minute discovery call later that week.
**Secondary (always extracted, even from "no" calls):** What eats their time? What did they complain about? What did they say "if only I had..." about?

The discovery call is where actual scoping happens. The first call is just qualification + booking.

## Phase 1 sprint targets

Volume target for the first week:
- **50 leads** in the dashboard by Sunday night
- **10 calls/day** Monday through Friday
- **15 conversations had** (not voicemail) over the week
- **3-5 discovery calls booked** by Friday

The 3-5 booking number is realistic. Cold-calling B2B from zero, with a portfolio but no warm intro, ~10% conversation-to-booking is decent first-week performance. If 5+, it's a strong start. If 0, the brief or pitch is wrong, not Sean.

**The success metric that's underrated:** 50 pain-point notes captured. This is the customer-discovery data that makes Phase 1 worth doing even if zero calls book. After 100 calls, patterns emerge. The product Sean eventually builds is hiding in this data.

## Sunday review ritual

Each Sunday evening (after the first week of calling), Sean should:

1. Open the leads list, filter by calls made that week
2. Read every pain-points field
3. Look for repeats — same complaint from 3+ different businesses
4. Write a short reflection: what's the most common frustration? What surprised you? What did people say they'd pay for?
5. Update the brief for next week's sprint based on what you learned

This ritual is what turns calling from "trying to make sales" into "running a market research operation that also makes sales."

## Notes for lead list construction

When Sean builds the 50-lead CSV on Sunday afternoon:

- Use Google Maps to search "[industry] near Chicago, IL" and similar
- Pin results to specific neighborhoods to enable geographic batching during calls (do all Logan Square calls Monday morning, all Lincoln Park Tuesday morning, etc.)
- Skim the website — does it look outdated? Is there an obvious gap (no online booking, no email signup, ancient design)? Note it in the lead notes.
- Look for the contact name if discoverable (About page, "team" page) — owner first name is huge for the opener
- Verify phone number is published and direct (not a generic "info@" route)
- Skip any business that's part of a chain
- Skip any that has obvious tech sophistication

The list doesn't need to be perfect. A bad lead caught on call 1 is faster than research time saved on call 0.

## What this brief becomes

This is the brief for the **first sprint**. After week 1, Sean rewrites it based on what he learned. The brief evolves with the calling experience. Successful brief = leads convert above target rate. Failed brief = no fits, no pain captured, no patterns visible.

Brief evolution is itself a skill. The targeting brief is a living document; this is just the v1.
