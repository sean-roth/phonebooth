# Discovery Call

15 minutes. Four questions. One close.

The goal is not to convince. The goal is to surface the trigger event, identify the most painful topic, and propose a single-module pilot.

## Open (2 minutes)

Thank them. Restate the cold call so context is fresh. Ask if anything's shifted.

> "Thanks for jumping on. When we talked Tuesday you mentioned [trigger they shared]. Anything change since then, or are we still in the same place?"

This does two things: confirms the trigger is real (people sometimes oversell on the cold call and walk it back here), and signals you remember them, which most cold callers don't.

## The four questions (8 minutes, ~2 min each)

Ask in order. Listen more than you talk. Take notes (or have Twilio recording running and let them know).

### 1. "Walk me through what training looks like for a new hire — day one to day fully cleared on the truck."

What you're listening for:
- Is there any formal process at all?
- Who delivers it (owner, foreman, nobody)?
- How long does it take?
- What do they hand new hires (binder, video, nothing)?
- Where do new hires actually learn the work — formal training or watching the senior guy?

The gap between "day one" and "cleared" is where the pain lives.

### 2. "Last time you had to show training records to anyone — inspector, insurance, GC — what did that look like?"

What you're listening for:
- Has it actually happened? (If yes, this is your trigger.)
- How long did it take to produce records?
- What did they end up showing (sign-in sheets, certificates, nothing)?
- Did it work, or did they get pushed back on?

If they say "it hasn't happened" — probe one level: "Are you bidding on work where you think it might?"

### 3. "If a crew member got hurt on a job tomorrow, could you prove they'd been trained on the right procedure?"

What you're listening for:
- Hesitation. Most owners hesitate here because the honest answer is "not really."
- The defensive answer: "Well, they signed the sheet." — that's the gap. A signed sheet doesn't prove understanding.
- Whether they've thought about litigation exposure.

Don't pile on if they look uncomfortable. The point of the question is for *them* to feel the gap, not for you to make them feel bad about it.

### 4. "Which topic do you think your guys are signing off on without really getting? The one that worries you?"

This is the killer question. Owners almost always have an answer ready — they know which topic is the weakest link. LOTO is common for electrical. Fall protection is common for any trade working at height. Hazcom for anyone with chemicals.

This topic is what you pilot.

## Playback and close (5 minutes)

### Play back what you heard

> "So here's what I'm hearing. You've got [current state]. The trigger is [what's pushing them — bid prequal, insurance, near-miss, etc.]. The weak spot you're worried about is [their answer to Q4]. Did I get that right?"

Let them confirm or correct. This is the moment they realize you actually listened, which is rare in B2B sales.

### The pilot pitch

> "Here's what I'd suggest. Single-module pilot on [their Q4 topic]. $3,500. I build it in a week from your existing SOP and any other source material you have. Your crew runs it. You see what your guys actually retain — and you get a verifiable training record for that one topic. If you want to do the rest of the required curriculum after, I knock $500 off the bundle."

Then stop. Whoever talks first loses.

### Possible responses

**"Yes, let's do it."** — Send a one-page agreement and a Stripe link the same day. Don't lose momentum. SOW is short: scope, deliverable, timeline, payment, what happens if they want changes.

**"Can we just do the whole thing?"** — Even better. Quote the starter program at $14k or full curriculum at $32–42k. Same SOW, bigger scope.

**"Let me think about it."** — Fine. "What would you need to know to make a yes/no decision?" That answer tells you the real objection. Common ones: needs to ask spouse/partner, wants to see the LOTO demo first, needs to align with their CSP. Address each, propose next step with a date.

**"It's more than I expected."** — "What were you thinking?" Listen. If they were thinking $500, they're not a fit yet — politely end. If they were thinking $1,500–$2,000, walk through the math: a single Level 2 custom-built module from their SOPs, with verified completion records, is $7,500–$22,500 in the broader market. You're priced at $3,500 because you're a small shop and they'd be early. If they still can't, end politely and put them in the demo-pipeline list.

## What success looks like

- 80% of discovery calls produce a yes, a clear next step, or an honest "not now, here's what would change that."
- 20% are not-fits and that's fine — you found out in 15 minutes instead of writing a proposal.
- Aim for 1 close per 3–5 discovery calls in month one. This improves with reps.

## What to send within 1 hour of the call

- Thank-you note (text or email — match how they prefer)
- LOTO demo link if they haven't seen it
- One-page summary of what was discussed and what you proposed
- SOW + Stripe link if they said yes

One hour is the sweet spot. Faster looks aggressive. Slower loses momentum.
