# Pricing

Reference for the cold call and discovery call. Don't negotiate against yourself.

## The tiers

### Pilot module — $3,500

One SOP, one module, 30–45 minutes of seat time. Three to five days to deliver. This is the "prove it works" offer. Easy first yes.

**Founding client variant: $2,500** for the first 3 clients only, in exchange for explicit permission to publish a case study with their company name, logo, and a quote.

### Starter program — $14,000–$18,000

3–4 modules covering whatever's most painful. For electrical: typically LOTO, Hazcom, Fall Protection, PPE. Two to three weeks. This will be your most common sale.

Quote $14k for clean scope; $18k when content is messy or there's a regulatory wrinkle.

### Full required curriculum — $32,000–$42,000

6–10 modules covering the full OSHA-required topic list for their trade. Four to six weeks. This is the contractor who's actively losing bids to safety prequal failures and decides to fix it permanently.

### Enterprise — $60,000+

Multi-site, multi-trade, custom LMS integration, bilingual (English/Spanish — huge for trades). Scoped individually. Don't expect this for the first six months but keep it on the menu — it makes the smaller prices look reasonable.

### Recurring — $200–$400/month

Hosting, audit trail maintenance, minor updates. **Don't bundle this into the project fee.** It should be a separate monthly line item starting at delivery.

- Pilot client: $200/month
- Starter program: $300/month
- Full curriculum: $400/month

This is how you don't end up rebuilding your client base every year. Three years of $300/month is more than the original starter program fee.

## How to quote on the call

When they ask "how much does it cost?":

> "Depends on scope. Single-module pilots run around $3,500 to prove the system works. Most contractors who do the full required curriculum invest somewhere between $15k and $40k depending on how many topics. There's also a small monthly for hosting and the audit trail. But I'd rather know what you're trying to solve before quoting — that's what the discovery call is for."

The casual reference to "$15k to $40k" does the heavy lifting. It signals you're not a freelance commodity. It pre-qualifies budget. It anchors them above the starter range so $14k feels reasonable.

Anyone who chokes on "$15k to $40k" is not a fit yet — you save yourself the discovery call.

## How to quote in the discovery call

After the four-question framework (see discovery-call.md), play back what you heard, identify the most painful topic, then:

> "Here's what I'd suggest. Single-module pilot on [topic]. $3,500. I build it in a week, your crew runs it, you decide if you want the rest. If yes, I knock $500 off the bundle."

Low risk, fast, lets them say yes without committing to a $14k+ decision.

If they're already showing strong intent ("we need this whole thing"), skip the pilot and quote the starter program directly.

## What the market actually charges

From 2026 industry sources (AllenComm, Chapman Alliance, eLearning Solutions Lab, Brandon Hall):

- Level 1 (basic): $5,000–$15,000 per finished hour
- Level 2 (interactive, branching): $15,000–$30,000 per finished hour
- Level 3 (simulations, scenario): $25,000–$50,000+ per finished hour

What you build is solidly Level 2. A 30–45 min Level 2 module "should" cost $7,500–$22,500 by industry math. You're pricing pilot modules at $3,500.

You're competitive but not insulting. Stay there until you have 5 closed clients, then raise.

## What you're competing against

- **Vector Solutions / ClickSafety**: $50k+ implementations, slow, generic content with custom skin. Better-known, worse-fit for small contractors.
- **Off-the-shelf OSHA video providers**: $200–$1,000/year subscriptions. Cheap and useless. Won't satisfy GC prequal or insurance requirements.
- **PowerPoint and a binder**: free and broken.

Your position: custom-built from their SOPs, fast, defensible audit trail, mid-market pricing. That's a strong position.

## What NOT to do

### Don't drop price when they hesitate

Hesitation is normal. Price is rarely the actual objection — usually it's trust or trigger event. If they're asking "is this worth it," that's a value conversation, not a price conversation. Drop your price and you've taught them the price was wrong to begin with, and they're now negotiating from a lower anchor.

### Don't bundle the recurring into the project fee

Separate line items. The audit trail has ongoing cost and ongoing value to them. Charge for it monthly.

### Don't forget the audit trail in the pitch

The Bitcoin-anchored OpenTimestamps verification is genuinely unique. Vector Solutions can't do this. ClickSafety can't do this. Your competitors can't fake training records that don't exist or alter records after the fact. Yours can be independently verified by anyone with a Bitcoin block explorer.

This is what insurance carriers and litigation attorneys eventually pay for. Don't bury it.

### Don't claim safety expertise

You're not a CSP or CIH. You don't write safety procedures. You take theirs and convert them. Saying this clearly is *better* positioning, not worse — it puts content liability where it legally already sits (with the client) and lets you focus on what you're actually expert in.

## How to flex if you need to

Never just discount. Three legitimate moves:

1. **Shrink scope** — fewer modules at the same per-module price. "Let's start with just LOTO and Hazcom for $7k instead of the four-module starter at $14k."
2. **Extend payment** — same total, paid over 6 months. "$14k starter, paid as $2,500 down and $1,900/month for six months."
3. **Run a single-module pilot first** — $3,500, then full bundle at standard rate if they like it.
