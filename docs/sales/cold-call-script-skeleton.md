# Cold Call Script — Skeleton

The structure, not the words. Fill in your own sentences. Read aloud as you go — if it sounds like you, it's right. If it sounds like a sales template, rewrite it.

Total target length: 60 seconds before they speak. Don't go past 75.

---

## Move 1 — Permission opener (10–15 seconds)

**The job of this move:** acknowledge the cold call out loud so they don't have to. Trade owners get pitched constantly; the ones who pretend they're not pitching are the ones that get hung up on. Naming the cold call honestly puts you in a different category in the first sentence.

**What it has to contain:**
- Confirm you have the right person (or ask for them)
- Your name
- That you're calling from Chicago (the 312 number is doing work — use it)
- An honest acknowledgment that this is unsolicited
- A small ask for time, with a number attached (15 seconds, 20 seconds, 30 seconds)

**What it must NOT do:**
- Fake rapport ("how's your day going")
- Pretend you know them ("we met at...")
- Disguise the call as something else ("I'm doing research...")

## Move 2 — Value statement (20–30 seconds)

**The job of this move:** tell them exactly what you do, in concrete terms, and why you're calling them specifically right now. Two halves: what + why-now.

### Half A — What you do (concrete, not abstract)

**What it has to contain:**
- The actual work — turning their existing safety procedures into training their crews complete
- Concrete topics, not categories ("LOTO, fall protection, hazcom" beats "safety training")
- The verifiable record / audit trail (this is your differentiator)

**What it must NOT do:**
- Abstract SaaS language ("interactive solutions," "platform," "ecosystem")
- Buzzwords from AI marketing ("AI-powered," "intelligent," "automated")
- Anything that sounds like a homepage hero section

### Half B — Why you're calling them right now (the trigger)

**The job of this half:** name a thing that's happening in their world that makes this call relevant. The two real triggers:
- GCs / public-bid jobs requiring documented training to bid
- Insurance carriers tying premiums to documented safety programs

**What it has to contain:**
- One or both triggers, named directly
- Phrasing that makes it about *them*, not about the world in general

**What it must NOT do:**
- Generic "in today's world" framing
- Made-up urgency ("limited spots," "this quarter only")

## Move 3 — The ask (10–15 seconds)

**The job of this move:** give them an open-ended question that lets them tell you whether the trigger is real for them. NOT a yes/no. Yes/no questions to a stranger get reflexive nos.

**What it has to contain:**
- A question that asks about something happening to them, not about how they feel
- Something specific enough that the answer tells you whether they're a fit
- Phrasing that lets a "no" be a clean exit, not a defeat

**What it must NOT do:**
- Pitch the product (you already did that)
- Ask if they're "interested" or "happy with their current solution" (closed, defensive)
- Try to book the meeting yet — that comes after they answer

**Examples of the shape (don't copy — make your own):**
- "Are you running into [trigger] right now, or is that not on the radar yet?"
- "What does training look like for your crews these days — formal, informal, somewhere in between?"
- "Last time a GC asked you for prequal docs, what did that look like?"

## Then — shut up

After the ask, silence. Don't fill it. Don't soften it. Don't add a postscript. Whoever talks first loses.

Count to four in your head if you have to.

## Test it before you dial

Read the filled-in script out loud three times.
- First read: see if you can get through it without choking on any phrase.
- Second read: see if it sounds like you'd actually say this to a stranger at a hardware store.
- Third read: see if you can do it without looking at the page.

If any of those three reads fails, edit the phrase that broke. One edit at a time.

When all three reads work, you're ready.

---

## What "sounds like you" actually means

The test isn't formality vs. casualness. The test is: would you cringe if someone you respected heard you say this on a call?

Phrases that pass this test for most people sound *plain*, not clever. Plain language is harder to write than clever language because there's nowhere to hide. The first version of any cold-call line is usually too clever; the third version is usually too formal; the fifth version is usually right.

Don't aim for charisma. Aim for "didn't sound like a salesperson."
