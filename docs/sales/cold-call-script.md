# Cold Call Script

Pull this up before dialing. Read it once, glance only during the call.

The whole call is about 14 seconds before they decide. Fast qualification is the goal: a fast hangup is better than a polite 2-minute waste.

---

## The opener

> "Good morning, this is Sean with SOPs Nobody Reads, training that gets done, can I borrow 30 seconds of your time?"

14 words. Brand name in beat two. Slogan in beat three. Ask in beat four. The slogan does the value statement's job — they hear "training" and either have pain or don't.

**Then stop talking.** Whoever talks first loses. Count to four if you have to.

## What happens next

### They hang up

That's the game. Fast no = fast information. The opener reached a decision moment; they decided. Move to the next dial without ruminating.

### They grunt acknowledgment / "go ahead"

Now run the rest. Confirm the name, then specifics:

> "Thanks, who am I speaking with?"

*They give name. Write it in phonebooth.*

> "Appreciate it, [name]. I take the SOPs and procedures you already have and turn them into actual training your crews run on a phone or tablet. About a week per topic. How are you handling training these days — binder, online, something else?"

Then listen.

### They sound like a gatekeeper (quiet office, not the decision-maker, casual answer)

Signals: woman in a quiet room when you expected a contractor on a site, someone who sounds like "office" not "shop," anyone who immediately defers to "the owner" or "my husband."

Don't burn the opener on them. Pivot:

> "Oh — is the owner around? Could I grab two minutes with him?"

Three branches from here:

- *"He's on a job"* / *"He's not in right now"* — "What's the best time to catch him?" Get a time. Call back at that time. Don't leave a message yet.
- *"Can I take a message?"* — "Sure. Sean from SOPs Nobody Reads. I'll try back later — just wanted to introduce myself." Short. Don't pitch the gatekeeper. Don't leave the number — they can't call back anyway (see Voicemail section).
- *"What's this regarding?"* — "Training. I work with small contractors on the OSHA side — building training from their own SOPs. Just two minutes with him would tell me if it's relevant." Concrete enough to be credible, vague enough that he decides, not her.

The gatekeeper is not the enemy. She's protecting his time. Respect that, don't try to bypass her, and she'll often hand him the phone.

### They ask what it is

> "I take the SOPs you already have and turn them into training your crews run on a phone or tablet, with a verifiable record. About a week per topic. Worth 30 more seconds?"

### They say "not interested" / "we're set"

> "Fair enough. The site has a 10-minute LOTO demo — sopsnobodyreads.com. If training ever bites you, you'll know who we are."

End call. They go in the future-pipeline list.

## After they answer about their training setup

Whatever they say is the conversation. You don't have to control it. Listen. Ask one or two follow-ups based on what they said. Then propose the discovery call.

### "We have a binder" / "the senior guy shows new hires" / "nothing formal"

> "Pretty common. Two things I'd ask — are you bidding on any commercial work where they're asking for proof of training? And what's your insurance carrier been saying lately about your safety program?"

If either trigger is real:

> "Worth 15 minutes to look at what you have and what your exposure is? I won't pitch you in that call. If it's a fit I'll tell you, if not I'll tell you that too."

### "We use ClickSafety / Vector / [generic OSHA video provider]"

> "Got it. Honest question — if a GC asked you tomorrow to walk through how your crew gets trained on LOTO, would the records hold up?"

If they hesitate, that's the opening. If confident, point them to the demo and move on.

### "We have an LMS already"

> "Fair enough. The site has a 10-minute LOTO demo built from a real contractor's SOPs — sopsnobodyreads.com. Different approach than what you've probably seen. No follow-up unless you reach out."

## Objections

### "Just send me info"

> "The fastest way is the demo on the site — sopsnobodyreads.com, takes 10 minutes to click through. Want to grab 15 minutes on my calendar after you've had a chance?"

Point to the site, then ask for the calendar slot. The site does the info-send better than an email would.

### "How much does it cost?"

> "Depends on scope. Single-module pilots run around $3,500 to prove the system works. Most contractors who do the full required curriculum invest somewhere between $15k and $40k depending on how many topics. There's also a small monthly for hosting and the audit trail. But I'd rather know what you're trying to solve before quoting — that's what the discovery call is for."

### "We're not interested"

> "Fair enough. The site has a 10-minute LOTO demo — sopsnobodyreads.com. If training ever bites you, you'll know who we are."

## Voicemail

**The 312 number is outbound-only.** Inbound callbacks don't route anywhere. Voicemail messages must point to the website as the response surface, not the phone number.

Target: 12–15 seconds.

> "Hi, this is Sean from SOPs Nobody Reads. We build OSHA training from your own SOPs — for GC prequal and insurance documentation. Take a look at sopsnobodyreads.com when you have a minute — there's a 10-minute LOTO demo on there that shows exactly what we do. Thanks."

One voicemail per prospect per month maximum.

**Delivery note:** *sopsnobodyreads.com* runs together if said fast. Slow down for the URL specifically. Say "SOPs Nobody Reads dot com" with a deliberate cadence, or repeat it: *"That's sopsnobodyreads.com."* The brand name is a sentence — readers parse it fine on a postcard but listeners need a beat.

### When to upgrade

When inbound volume becomes a real loss (probably week 3–4), configure the Twilio number to forward to Sean's cell. Then voicemail can include a callback number as an option alongside the site.

## If they ask "are you a safety expert?"

> "No, that's your domain. Your CSP, your insurance carrier's risk consultant, OSHA's regulations — that's where the safety expertise lives. I'm an instructional designer who specializes in trade environments. I take the procedures you already have and turn them into training your crews actually complete and remember. Source of truth is your SOPs. I'm the conversion layer."

## The fast qualification principle

Validated May 11, 2026. First real cold call: prospect heard the full 14-word opener and hung up. Total call time: ~14 seconds.

This is the win condition for a not-fit prospect. A polite 2-minute conversation that ends in "send info" with no real interest wastes 8x more time AND gives you a fake email to chase. A fast hangup is efficient: it confirms the opener reached a decision moment, and it returns you to the next dial without rumination.

Don't soften the opener to avoid hangups. Hangups are the system working. The opener should reach a decision point fast, and most decisions on a cold call are no. Optimize for *fast no*, not *polite no*.
