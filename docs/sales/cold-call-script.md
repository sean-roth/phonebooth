# Cold Call Script

Pull this up before dialing. Read it once, glance only during the call.

The whole call is about 45–60 seconds before they speak. Three moves. Then shut up.

---

## The script

**Pickup:**

> "Hi, this is Sean from SOPs Nobody Reads. Who am I speaking with?"

*They give a name. Write it in phonebooth.*

> "Thanks, [name]. I'll be straight — this is a cold call. Got 30 seconds?"

*They say yes (or grunt acknowledgment). If they say no, jump to "Just send me info" objection below.*

> "I take the SOPs and procedures you already have and turn them into actual training your crews run on a phone or tablet. About a week per topic. How are you handling training these days — binder, online, something else?"

**Then stop talking.** Whoever talks first loses. Count to four if you have to.

---

## Why each piece is there

**"Who am I speaking with?"** — Small commitment-getter. Once they've given their name, hang-up rate drops. Also solves the lead-prep gap where you don't always have a name.

**"I'll be straight — this is a cold call."** — Trade owners get pitched constantly by people who pretend they're not pitching. Naming the call honestly puts you in a different category in the first sentence. This is the trust move. Don't drop it.

**"I take your SOPs and turn them into training your crews run on a phone or tablet."** — Concrete, not abstract. Avoid "interactive modules," "platform," "AI-powered." Plain language wins.

**"About a week per topic."** — Specific time claim. Beats "fast" or "quickly" every time.

**"How are you handling training these days — binder, online, something else?"** — Open-ended. Lets them describe their world instead of admitting a flaw to a stranger. Whatever they say is the next move.

---

## After they answer

Whatever they say is the conversation. You don't have to control it from here. Listen. Ask one or two follow-ups based on what they said. Then propose the discovery call.

### "We have a binder" / "we just have the senior guy show new hires" / "kind of nothing formal"

> "Pretty common. Two things I'd ask — are you bidding on any commercial work where they're asking for proof of training? And what's your insurance carrier been saying lately about your safety program?"

If either trigger is real, propose the discovery call:

> "Worth 15 minutes to look at what you have and what your exposure is? I won't pitch you in that call. If it's a fit I'll tell you, if not I'll tell you that too."

### "We use ClickSafety / Vector / [generic OSHA video provider]"

> "Got it. Honest question — if a GC asked you tomorrow to walk through how your crew gets trained on LOTO, would the records hold up?"

If they hesitate, that's the opening. If they sound confident, send the demo and move on:

> "All good. Mind if I send you our 10-minute LOTO demo? Different approach — built from your own SOPs with a verifiable record. If it ever comes up, you'll know who to call."

### "We have an LMS already" / "we're set"

> "Fair enough. Mind if I send you our 10-minute LOTO demo so you've seen it? Built from the contractor's own SOPs, with a record that holds up if OSHA or insurance asks. No follow-up unless you reach out."

Get email. Move on. They go in the future-pipeline list.

---

## Objections

### "Just send me info"

> "Happy to. What's the best email? I'll send our 10-minute LOTO demo — that's the best look at what we do. Cool if I grab 15 minutes on your calendar after you've had a chance to click through?"

Send it AND book it. Information without a calendar slot is a graveyard.

### "How much does it cost?"

> "Depends on scope. Single-module pilots run around $3,500 to prove the system works. Most contractors who do the full required curriculum invest somewhere between $15k and $40k depending on how many topics. There's also a small monthly for hosting and the audit trail. But I'd rather know what you're trying to solve before quoting — that's what the discovery call is for."

Don't dodge. Give the range. Reframe to discovery.

### "We're not interested"

> "Fair enough. Can I send the demo anyway? Real OSHA course, takes 10 minutes to click through. If training ever bites you, you'll know who we are. No follow-up otherwise."

Almost no one says no to a one-shot demo with no follow-up promise. Email goes in the pipeline.

## Voicemail

Under 20 seconds. The 312 number on caller ID does the work — don't say "calling from Chicago."

> "Hi, this is Sean from SOPs Nobody Reads. I work with small electrical contractors on safety training built from your own SOPs — the kind that holds up for OSHA and GC prequal docs. Wanted to see if that's something you're running into. My number is [number] — happy to send a 10-minute demo either way. Thanks."

Don't leave more than one voicemail per prospect per month.

---

## If they ask "are you a safety expert?"

> "No, that's your domain. Your CSP, your insurance carrier's risk consultant, OSHA's regulations — that's where the safety expertise lives. I'm an instructional designer who specializes in trade environments. I take the procedures you already have and turn them into training your crews actually complete and remember. Source of truth is your SOPs. I'm the conversion layer."

This positions correctly and removes content liability from you.

## Recording review (4:00 PM CT)

Listen to:
- Your 3 best calls
- Your 3 worst calls
- 3 random

Twenty minutes. Adjust one thing per day. The voice doesn't lie.

What to listen for:
- Are you rushing the value statement?
- Are you hedging on the price question?
- Are you talking through their "yes" because you're nervous?
- Where do you hear yourself apologizing for calling?

Fix one thing. Run it again tomorrow.
