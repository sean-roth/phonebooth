# Cold Call Script

The whole call is 60 seconds. Three moves. Then you shut up.

## The opener (15 seconds)

> "Hi, is this [name]? My name's Sean from SOPs Nobody Reads, calling from Chicago. I'll be straight — this is a cold call. Can I take 30 seconds to tell you why I called and you can decide if it's worth more?"

The honesty is the unlock. Most owners say yes because nobody else opens like this. If they say no, immediately pivot to the demo offer (see Objections below).

**Note on the 312 number.** You're calling Chicago contractors from a 312 number. They assume local. Don't downplay it. "Calling from Chicago" is fully truthful and buys you another five seconds of attention.

## The value (30 seconds)

> "We turn the safety procedures you already have — LOTO, fall protection, whatever's in your binder — into actual training your crews complete on a phone or tablet, with a verifiable record. I'm calling small electrical contractors specifically because two things are happening right now. GCs and government jobs are asking for documented training to bid, and a binder with signatures isn't cutting it. And insurance carriers are tying premiums to whether you can prove your safety program holds up."

Swap "electrical" for whatever trade you're calling. Keep the two-trigger framing (GC prequal + insurance pressure) — those are the actual buying triggers.

## The ask (15 seconds)

> "Two questions. Are you bidding on any work right now where they're asking for proof of training? And what's your current setup — binder, LMS, or kind of nothing formal?"

Then stop talking. Whoever talks first loses.

## Branches

### "Yes, we lost a bid / they're asking and we don't have it"

You're in.

> "That's exactly why I called. Worth 15 minutes to look at your binder and tell you what gets you defensible? I won't sell you in that call — if it's a fit I'll tell you, if not I'll tell you that too."

Book the discovery call. Send Calendly link via SMS or email immediately after the call.

### "We have an LMS / Vector Solutions / ClickSafety / generic OSHA video provider"

> "Got it. How long does a new hire take to get through it? And honest question — if OSHA walked in tomorrow and asked your guys to walk through your hazcom procedure from memory, would they be able to?"

Listen. If they sound confident, send the demo and move on. If they sound shaky, that's your opening.

### "Not bidding that kind of work, no one's asking"

> "Understood — most contractors I talk to run into this when they go after bigger jobs. I'd rather not pitch you on a problem you don't have. Can I send you our LOTO demo so you know who to call if it ever comes up?"

Get email. This is your future pipeline. They'll remember when the trigger event hits.

### "We don't have any formal training"

> "That's pretty common. What's your insurance carrier saying these days — any pushback or premium changes?"

Listen. Almost all of them have insurance pressure right now.

> "Worth 15 minutes to look at what you have and what your exposure is? Even if we don't work together, you'll walk away clearer."

## The three objections you'll hear most

### "Just send me information"

> "Happy to — what's the best email? I'll send our LOTO demo, that's the best look at what we actually do. Cool if I grab 15 minutes on the calendar after you've had a chance to click through?"

Send it AND book it. Information without a calendar slot is a graveyard.

### "How much does it cost?"

> "Depends on scope. Single-module pilots run around $3,500 to prove the system works. Most contractors who do the full required curriculum invest somewhere between $15k and $40k depending on how many topics. There's also a small monthly for hosting and the audit trail. But I'd rather know what you're trying to solve before quoting — that's what the discovery call is for."

Don't dodge the price question — trade owners hate that. Give the range and reframe to discovery.

### "We're not interested"

> "Fair enough. Can I send the demo anyway? It's a real OSHA course, takes 10 minutes to click through. If training ever bites you, you'll know who we are. No follow-up otherwise."

Almost no one says no to a one-shot demo with no follow-up promise. That email goes in the pipeline.

## Voicemail

Keep it under 20 seconds.

> "Hi [name], this is Sean from SOPs Nobody Reads in Chicago. I work with small electrical contractors on safety training that holds up for OSHA and GC prequal docs. Wanted to see if that's something you're running into. My number is [number] — happy to send a 10-minute demo either way. Thanks."

Don't leave more than one voicemail per prospect per month.

## The credibility line (when asked who you are)

> "I'm an instructional designer who specializes in trade environments. I'm not the safety expert — your CSP, your insurance carrier's risk consultant, OSHA's regulations are. I take the procedures you already have and turn them into training your crews actually complete and remember. Source of truth is your SOPs. I'm the conversion layer."

This is better than fudging credentials. It puts content liability where it belongs (with them, where it already legally lives) and lets you focus on what you actually do.

## The recording review discipline

Twilio records every call. At end of day, listen to:
- The 3 calls that went best
- The 3 calls that went worst
- 3 random

Twenty minutes of audio review beats two hours of script editing. Adjust one thing per day. The voice doesn't lie.

What to listen for:
- Are you rushing the value section?
- Are you hedging on the price question?
- Are you talking through their "yes" because you're nervous?
- Where do you hear yourself apologizing for calling?

Fix one thing. Run it again tomorrow.
