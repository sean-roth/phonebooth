# Brand & Messaging

The slogan, where it deploys, why each word is there, and the open A/B tests.

## The brand name

**SOPs Nobody Reads.**

This is settled. Don't change it. The name does work passively (one near-sale was triggered by name resonance alone) and it inverts cleanly with the slogan to form a complete pitch.

## The slogan — current candidates (A/B testing)

Two finalists. Both pass the cognitive-flow test: brand name → "training" appears within two beats → specifics follow.

### Variant A — "SOPs Nobody Reads — training with receipts."

- **Strengths:** Distinctive, memorable, points at the audit trail without naming it. High stopping-power on postcards. "Receipts" is current vernacular for "proof" and lands hard.
- **Risks:** May read as too informal to older trade owners. Slight slang risk.
- **Best deployed on:** Postcards, LinkedIn header, business cards, t-shirts, any visual surface where stopping-power matters more than universality.

### Variant B — "SOPs Nobody Reads — training that gets done."

- **Strengths:** Universal across ages. Plain language. Inverts the brand name cleanly (SOPs Nobody Reads → training that does get done).
- **Risks:** Slightly forgettable. Nothing in it snags the ear.
- **Best deployed on:** The cold call value statement, voicemails, email signatures, surfaces where plainness matters more than distinctiveness.

### A/B test plan

- **Postcard tier-1 test (50 hand-addressed pieces):** Use Variant A. Track inbound and warm-call lift.
- **Cold call A/B (week 2 of dialing):** First 5 calls each day with Variant B in the value statement. Next 5 calls with current value statement (no slogan). Track conversation rate. Run for 5 dial days = 50 calls of each.
- **Decision date:** End of week 3 of dialing. Pick one variant for each surface based on data, not feel.

## The brand-name commitment-getter (call-only)

The opener change driven by the slogan work:

> "Hi, this is Sean from SOPs Nobody Reads — we build training that gets done. Who am I speaking with?"

This front-loads the pitch into 8 seconds and lets prospects self-select faster. Faster nos free time for faster yeses.

This is **not yet committed to** — it's the call-side variant being tested. See `cold-call-script.md` for the current production opener.

## Why "training" appears in the second beat

The cognitive flow on a cold call goes: brand name → category recognition → specifics. If the prospect doesn't know what category you're in by second 3, they're already half-disengaged. Putting "training" immediately after the brand name gives them the mental frame before your value statement lands. The pitch then has context instead of having to create it.

This is why slogans like "custom training, fast" or "your procedures, made learnable" lose to "training with receipts" or "training that gets done" — the latter put the category word first.

## Why not "AI-powered training" or "instructional design for trades"

- **"AI-powered"** is the most overclaimed phrase in 2026 B2B marketing. Trade owners' pattern-matchers flag it as filler. It also invites the wrong objection ("I don't want AI training my crew").
- **"Instructional design"** is jargon. Trade owners don't know the term and don't care.
- **"Custom"** matters but doesn't need to be in the slogan — the value statement carries it.
- **"Fast"** rejected: vague, overclaimed, invites the "fast but is it good" objection.

## Visual identity

Not yet locked in. Decisions to make before postcard tier-1 print:

- Typography: clean sans-serif. Avoid construction-themed fonts (hard hats, hammers, "trade-y" cliches). The brand should look like a serious B2B product, not a Yelp ad for a contractor.
- Color: muted, professional. Not safety-orange or hi-vis yellow — that's exactly the cliche to avoid. A deep navy, charcoal, or muted red would distinguish from the competition.
- Logo: wordmark only for now. Custom mark can come month 4+ if branding budget allows.

## What the brand should NOT do

- Pretend to be a safety expert. The brand is the *conversion layer*, not the safety authority.
- Use stock photos of crews in hard hats. Cliche, doesn't differentiate.
- Use the word "solutions." Ever.
- Adopt a cute mascot. Trade owners don't want fun; they want competent.

## The brand thesis, in one sentence

*A serious instructional-design product for small trade contractors who need their crews trained and their training documented — sold by a person who isn't pretending to be a safety expert, with proof-of-completion that holds up if anyone asks.*

That's the position. Everything visual, every slogan, every piece of collateral should pass the test of fitting that sentence.
