# Lead Targeting

How to select prospects for cold calls. Supersedes the granular targeting criteria in `docs/sales/target-industries.md` — that doc keeps the higher-level "which industries" focus; this doc handles the within-industry profile selection.

## The core insight

Targeting criteria optimize for one of three things, and they're often in tension:

1. **Novelty in the inbox** — how likely is the prospect to actually take the call
2. **Fit for the product** — how likely is the prospect to actually need what's being sold
3. **Closure likelihood** — how likely is the prospect to actually buy if interested

Anti-signal targeting (broken website, sparse reviews, low polish) optimizes hard for #1 and partially for #2. It does *not* predict #3. Polished targeting optimizes for #3 but punishes #1.

The first 100 calls should optimize for #1 and #2. After that, closure data dictates the next 200.

## The three target profiles

### Anti-signal profile (70% of the first 100 calls)

The current working hypothesis. Owner-operator who runs the business off a phone, prefers job sites to office work, hasn't been pitched aggressively because vendors don't find them.

**Selection criteria:**
- 1–25 employees (estimated from review patterns, team mentions, or photo signals)
- Broken website link OR no website OR a website that hasn't been touched since pre-2020
- Fewer than 50 Google reviews (often much fewer)
- Mixed reviews are fine — even helpful — but require at least one positive review in the past 6 months as a "still operating" signal
- Active business: most recent review within the past 12 months, ideally past 6
- Based in Cook County, IL (or adjacent counties for spillover)

**Hypothesis about this segment:**
- High call pickup rate (they answer their own phone)
- High novelty (vendors haven't found them)
- High training-gap probability (the disorganization on the web likely extends to training records)
- *Unknown* deal closure rate — could be high (real pain, decision speed) or low (no budget, can't execute)

**What this segment teaches:**
- Whether the pitch lands in plain language
- What objections sound like from blunt prospects
- Whether the trigger events (GC prequal, insurance pressure) actually surface in real conversation

### Mid-signal profile (20% of the first 100 calls)

The middle of the bell curve. Functional but not polished.

**Selection criteria:**
- 5–40 employees
- Working website that loads, but feels like it was last designed in 2018–2021
- 20–80 Google reviews
- Mostly positive but with at least one or two visible complaints
- Some sign of office function (a real contact form, an actual business address, listed hours)
- Cook County or adjacent

**Hypothesis about this segment:**
- Moderate call pickup rate (might have an office line with a gatekeeper)
- Moderate novelty (some vendors find them, but not all)
- Highest probability of actually buying — they have enough infrastructure to execute and enough pain to need help
- Best ROI per discovery call if they convert

**What this segment teaches:**
- Whether the brand resonates with prospects who've been pitched before
- What the proposal-to-close cycle actually looks like
- Whether the price points (pilot $3,500, starter $14k) land correctly

### Polished profile (10% of the first 100 calls)

Sophisticated, marketing-aware shops.

**Selection criteria:**
- 10–50 employees
- Clean modern website (post-2022 design)
- 50+ Google reviews, mostly 5-star
- Visible marketing investment (case studies, testimonials, project photography, social presence)
- Possibly already on commercial-bid platforms (Avetta, ISN — sometimes mentioned on site)
- Cook County preferred but can include adjacent counties

**Hypothesis about this segment:**
- Lower call pickup (gatekeepers, voicemail)
- Lower novelty (vendors find them; they've been pitched)
- May already have an LMS or training vendor
- *Highest* deal size potential if they convert (would likely go straight to full curriculum tier)

**What this segment teaches:**
- What sophisticated objections sound like
- Whether the audit trail differentiator actually beats existing LMS solutions
- Whether the brand can punch above its weight against ClickSafety / Vector Solutions in a head-to-head

## Why all three matter for the first 100

Calling only the anti-signal segment will produce a distorted view of "what works." A 10% conversion rate on 50 anti-signal calls doesn't tell you whether that's because of the segment, the script, the pitch, or the brand. Mixing the segments lets the data be interpretable.

The 70/20/10 split is also emotionally protective. The anti-signal segment is easier to call (low stakes, blunt prospects, less rejection sting). Starting each day with anti-signal calls and ending with mid-signal/polished calls builds confidence into the harder ones. Don't reverse the order.

## What anti-signals predict — and don't

| Anti-signal | What it likely predicts | What it does NOT predict |
|---|---|---|
| Broken website | Owner doesn't prioritize office work | Whether the business is financially healthy |
| Few Google reviews | Doesn't market actively / doesn't ask for reviews | Whether the business has steady work |
| No recent reviews | Could mean inactive OR could mean repeat customer base | Whether the owner picks up the phone |
| Mixed reviews | Inconsistent service delivery | Whether the owner is open to fixing it |
| Owner-only operation | Decision-maker is reachable | Whether they have budget for $3,500+ |

The pattern: anti-signals are good *call selection* filters but unreliable *budget/closure* filters. Don't conflate them.

## What to actually filter out

Genuine disqualifiers — these are different from "anti-signals":

- **No reviews at all + no website** — too uncertain whether they're operating
- **Reviews all 1–2 stars** — operationally broken in a way training won't fix
- **Most recent review >18 months ago** — likely defunct
- **Listed as "permanently closed" on Google** — obvious
- **Brand new (<1 year)** — usually not buying yet
- **Franchise or corporate parentage** — wrong decision-maker
- **>50 employees** — has an LMS; different sale, different cycle

These cut the list before the 70/20/10 split is applied.

## The trigger events override everything

If a prospect mentions any of these in a call regardless of segment, treat them as a hot lead:

1. *"We just lost a bid because we couldn't show training records."*
2. *"Our insurance just went up / EMR went up."*
3. *"OSHA was just here."*
4. *"We had a [near-miss / incident] last [time period].*
5. *"We're trying to bid on [bigger commercial work / public contracts]."*
6. *"GC asked us for [Avetta / ISN / safety prequal docs]."*

Trigger events predict closure better than any segmentation. A trigger-event prospect in the anti-signal segment is hotter than a no-trigger prospect in the polished segment.

## Review after 100 calls

Mandatory review at the 100-call mark. Look at:

- Pickup rate by segment
- Conversation rate by segment
- Discovery-call conversion rate by segment
- Trigger-event surface rate by segment
- Time-per-call by segment
- Subjective sense of which calls felt productive

The hypothesis going in: mid-signal converts highest; anti-signal teaches fastest; polished produces longest sales cycles but biggest deals.

If the data confirms the hypothesis, shift the next 200 calls to a 30/50/20 split (less anti-signal, more mid-signal, more polished).

If the data contradicts the hypothesis — for example, if anti-signal converts highest — stay with the current weighting and write down *why* you think the prediction was wrong before adjusting.

Either way, don't change the split before 100 calls. Smaller samples produce noise, not signal.

## When this doc gets updated

After the 100-call review. Replace the hypotheses with observed data. Replace the placeholder splits with empirically-derived ones. Add a new "validated criteria" section based on which signals actually predicted closure.

Don't update this doc before 100 calls. The hypotheses are the experimental conditions; changing them mid-experiment produces uninterpretable data.

## On industry mixing

The 27 manual leads were drawn from multiple trades. That's fine for the first 100 — segment diversity within trade is more important than trade diversity for now. After 100 calls, if one trade is dramatically outperforming, narrow to that trade for the next 200.

Current target trades (from `docs/sales/target-industries.md`):

- Electrical contractors (commercial-focused, not residential)
- Mechanical / HVAC contractors (commercial)
- Specialty trades on commercial sites (fireproofing, insulation, sheet metal, glazing, demolition)
- Landscaping (added based on manual prep — physical work, novel sites, low software adoption, fits the friction-creates-pain thesis)
- Painters, drywall contractors, tile/stone/concrete (less-tooled trades)

Don't add new trades to the mix during the first 100 calls. Stay with the current set, then expand based on data.
