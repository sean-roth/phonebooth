# Design Kit

**Status: Stub.** Not for execution. Reference for the future website revamp, direct mail print run, and business card production. Decisions get made when there's a budget and a deliverable, not now.

The purpose of this doc is to prevent accidental brand drift in the meantime — if a postcard mockup or LinkedIn banner has to be produced before a real designer is engaged, the defaults below keep it from fragmenting the brand.

## What this is and isn't

- **Is:** Working defaults to keep visual decisions consistent across surfaces until a real design pass happens.
- **Is not:** A finished design system. Don't pay a designer based on this. Don't print 1,000 postcards based on this without a design pass first.
- **Authority:** Anchored to the brand thesis in `brand-and-messaging.md`. If any decision here drifts from that thesis, the thesis wins.

## Brand thesis recap

*A serious instructional-design product for small trade contractors who need their crews trained and their training documented — sold by a person who isn't pretending to be a safety expert, with proof-of-completion that holds up if anyone asks.*

Every visual decision should pass the test of fitting this sentence.

## Color system (placeholder)

Working defaults. Real palette gets locked in by a designer.

- **Primary: Deep navy** — approximately `#1B2845` or similar. Conveys seriousness and credibility without reading as cold or corporate.
- **Background: Warm white** — approximately `#F7F4EE`. Avoids the sterile-tech look of pure white; signals approachability.
- **Accent: Undecided.** Two candidates: a desaturated brick red (around `#9B3A28`) or a deep moss green (around `#4A5D3A`). Pick one and stick with it; don't use both.
- **Text: Charcoal** — approximately `#2A2A2A`. Not pure black; easier to read at length.

**Avoid at all costs:**

- Safety orange (`#FF6B00` and variants)
- Hi-vis yellow (`#FFF400` and variants)
- "Construction red" (bright saturated reds, especially with black)
- Pure black (too aggressive for the brand position)
- Any neon or fluorescent
- Any palette that reads as "safety industry cliche"

## Typography (placeholder)

Working defaults. Replace at real design pass.

- **Headlines: Inter** (Google Font, free, clean modern sans-serif). Weights 600–800 for impact.
- **Body: Inter** (same family, lighter weights 400–500). Or **Source Serif Pro** if headline/body contrast is desired.
- **Monospace (if needed):** JetBrains Mono or IBM Plex Mono.

**Avoid:**

- Anything condensed or industrial-styled
- Anything decorative
- Construction-themed fonts (anything that comes up when you search "construction font")
- Stenciled or military-styled types
- Anything that reads as "trade-y" rather than "professional service"

## What the brand looks like vs. doesn't

### Looks like

- A serious B2B software product
- An instructional design firm's collateral
- A muted, professional, intentional aesthetic
- Clean layouts with generous white space
- Confident typography over decorative imagery

### Doesn't look like

- A contractor's flyer
- A safety equipment catalog
- A "trades-y" advertisement with crews in hard hats
- A stock-photo-driven marketing piece
- A typical OSHA-training-vendor website (boring blue and yellow, generic stock crews, dated layout)
- Anything that could be mistaken for a competitor like ClickSafety or Vector Solutions

The negative space is more useful than the positive at this stage. Prevent bad early decisions; positive direction comes from the designer.

## What the brand never uses

- Stock photos of construction crews in hard hats
- Clipart or icon-pack illustrations
- Construction cliche imagery (toolbelts, blueprints, hammers, hard hats as decoration)
- A mascot
- The phrases banned in the brand skill ("solutions," "empower," etc.)
- Hi-vis or safety-industry color schemes

## When to upgrade to a real designer

Trigger conditions — act on whichever comes first:

- **Before any physical print run that costs more than $300.** Bad design becomes expensive at scale.
- **Before three closed clients.** The brand starts carrying weight; sloppy collateral becomes a credibility tax.
- **Before first $10k month.** Revenue can support the investment.
- **Before the website revamp.** The site is the most-seen surface; it should be designed properly before it's redesigned.

Budget expectation for first designer engagement: $2,000–$5,000 for a starter brand identity package (logo refinement, color, type, basic templates for 2–3 surfaces). Higher-end design firms will quote $10k+; for a brand at this stage, mid-range is fine.

## What to ask the designer to deliver

When the trigger fires, the brief should include:

- Logo system (wordmark primary, possibly a mark for compact uses)
- Color palette locked in (primary, secondary, neutral, accent — with hex, RGB, CMYK, and Pantone equivalents)
- Typography system (display, body, monospace if needed, with licensing notes)
- Templates for the surfaces in current production (postcard, business card, email signature, LinkedIn banner at minimum)
- A one-page brand guide that captures the above for future contractors/freelancers

Don't ask for an entire "brand book" or 50-page identity manual. Overscoped design engagements waste money and produce documents nobody reads. Ironic, given the company name.

## Open questions for the future design pass

- Should there be a custom logo mark in addition to the wordmark, or is the wordmark sufficient?
- Is the accent color brick red, moss green, or something else?
- Should the typography pair use serif body type for warmth, or stay all-sans for modern feel?
- How does the brand work in a sans-color, monochrome context (e.g., a black-and-white postcard)?
- What does a 4x6 postcard layout look like with all elements at production scale?

These are decisions for the designer, not Claude. Captured here so they don't get forgotten when the engagement happens.
