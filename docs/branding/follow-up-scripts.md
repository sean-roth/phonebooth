# Follow-Up Scripts — The Maybes Layer

*SOPs Nobody Reads · companion to `cold-call-pitch.md`, `segment-call-scripts.md`, `discovery-questions.md`, `after-the-yes-runbook.md` · June 2026*

**What this is:** cold calling mostly produces neither yes nor no — it produces callbacks, sent demos, passed-along referrals, and quiet inboxes. That's where most of the pipeline's value lives, and until now there was no script for any of it. There is no improvising the second touch either.

**The standing rule:** every conversation ends with a scheduled next touch, logged in the tracker before the next dial. No exceptions. A lead without a next touch is a lead you've decided to lose.

**The order-of-operations rule:** follow-ups are the FIRST dials of the morning block, before any cold dial. Warm leads decay by the day; cold lists don't. (Bonus: starting warm fixes the first-call-is-the-worst-call problem — you open the block talking to someone who already knows your name.)

---

## 1. The morning-of text (booked discovery — from the Mint 312 number)

> Morning [Name] — Sean Roth. We're on for [time] today, fifteen minutes about how you bring new people up to speed. Talk then.

No question mark anywhere. A question invites rescheduling.

## 2. The promised callback ("call me around noon" — the Oscar/Aldo play)

Call at the time he named, not near it. First sentence proves you listened:

> [Name] — Sean. You said to catch you around [time]. Still got two minutes?

Then one line of re-anchor, then the ask:

> Quick recap — I build onboarding modules from your own procedures. What I'd want is fifteen minutes to ask four questions about how you bring people up to speed. When's good this week?

If he's slammed: get a specific next time before you're off the phone. "I'll try you next week" is not a next touch. "Thursday at 10" is.

## 3. The referral chase (the gatekeeper-ally play — the Mary/STS thread)

She passed it up the chain. She's the asset; call HER back, 3–4 business days later:

> Hi [Mary] — Sean, we spoke [day] about the onboarding modules. Did that make it to [owner's] desk? ... Anything I can send that'd make it easier to pass along?

If it stalled: offer the one-line version she can forward — the website and a single sentence. If she gives you the owner's direct line or a time he's in the office, you just converted a gatekeeper into a referrer. Thank her by name. Log everything.

## 4. The demo follow-up (demo sent → call three days later)

The demo send is a reason to call again, not an end state.

> [Name] — Sean. I sent over that lockout/tagout module on [day]. Did you get a chance to click through it?

**If yes:** > What stood out? ... [listen] ... Want to see what one built from YOUR procedures looks like? That's the pilot — one topic, $3,500, delivered in a week.

**If no:** > No sweat — it's thirty minutes on your phone. I'll check back Friday. [And then actually check back Friday.]

One demo, two follow-up calls max. After that, 60-day lane.

## 5. The no-show recovery (booked discovery, didn't pick up)

No guilt. Guilt kills the thread; busyness is the assumption:

> [Name] — Sean. We had [time] on the calendar — I'm guessing the day got away from you, it happens. Want to grab another fifteen this week, or should I check back next month?

Two outs, both scheduled. Whatever he picks goes in the tracker. One no-show is a busy man; two no-shows is a soft no — move to the 60-day lane without resentment.

## 6. The recap-email silence (he said yes, the payment didn't land — day 5)

Assume buried, not cold feet. The job site pulls him under; that was always the risk the same-day email exists to beat.

> [Name] — Sean. Good talk last week. I sent the recap with the payment link — wanted to make sure it didn't get buried. Any questions on it, or has timing shifted?

**If timing shifted:** > Totally fine. What makes sense — kick off [next month], or should I check back in the fall?

One call + one email re-send, then 60-day lane. Chasing harder than this trades the honest-broker position for one deal. Don't.

## 7. The 60-day re-dial (soft no, circumstances change)

**Rule: the call must carry one new reason to exist.** Without it you're the vendor who didn't hear no. With it you're the guy who remembered.

> [Name] — Sean. We talked back in [June] — you [weren't hiring / had it covered] then. Reason I'm calling back: [the new thing]. Worth those fifteen minutes now?

New things that qualify: peak season is now visible (warehouse), turn season is on top of her (PM, spring/summer), you delivered a module for a company like his (the moment you have ONE client, every 60-day re-dial gets warmer), a new module topic that matches his earlier objection.

## 8. The conversion touch (pilot delivered + 2 weeks — the tier-2 seed)

This is the highest-revenue phone call in the entire system. The runbook plants the seed at delivery ("what's the next topic?"); this is the watering:

> [Name] — Sean. How's the module landing with the crew? ... [listen — this is also your testimonial, write it down verbatim] ... What's the topic the new guys still get wrong?

His answer to that last question is the next module. If he names one: > That's exactly the kind of thing the next module covers. Want me to scope it? 

If the pilot was hosted rather than SCORM, he already told you he's a program buyer — this call is expected, not intrusive.
