# Discovery Questions — 15-Minute Call

## Frame

You're not pitching. You're learning enough to decide if it's a fit and tell them either way. The contractor has been pitched a thousand times — he's never been told no by a vendor with respect. The honesty is the position.

The four questions are about his business, not about his training program. He wants to talk about his business. He doesn't want to talk about training.

## The four questions

### 1. How are you bringing on new hires right now? Walk me through what happens from the day someone accepts the offer to their first month on the job.

**Why:** Puts him in narrative mode. He tells you what actually happens, not what's in the handbook. You'll learn shop size, crew structure, who handles training informally, where the gaps are — without having to ask any of that directly.

**Listening for:**
- Who's responsible for the new hire's first week
- What materials exist (binder, slides, nothing)
- Where the breakdowns happen
- Resigned vs frustrated. Frustrated is buying. Resigned is not buying yet — he's adapted to the pain.

### 2. What's pushing you to look at this right now?

**Why:** The trigger. Tells you the timeline, urgency, and budget shape. Lost bid, insurance call, OSHA visit, specific new hire who didn't work out, GC asking for documentation.

**Listening for:**
- Specificity. Vague ("we should probably get more organized") is not buying. Specific ("Henderson Construction asked for our training records last month") is buying.
- The trigger sets the deadline. *How long do I have before this becomes urgent enough to matter to him?*

### 3. When a new guy joins your crew, what's the most important thing he needs to know in the first week? And what do guys typically get wrong when they don't know it yet?

**Why:** This is the load-bearing question. It orients the entire engagement if it goes forward. Also tests whether he has a real point of view about his own work. The contractors worth working with do.

**Listening for:**
- Can he answer with specifics? "Safety, mostly" is empty. "Knowing which conduit to pull for which application, because the spec sheets don't tell you and the older guys do" is a real customer.
- The story is the seed of the first module. Write it down verbatim if you can.

### 4. What have you tried, and what got in the way?

**Why:** Tells you what he's already ruled out and his actual constraints. Surfaces price expectations and patience for implementation.

**Listening for:**
- Tried nothing → earlier in his thinking than he sounds. May not be ready to buy yet.
- Tried cheap (ClickSafety, OSHA Training Today) and bounced → good. He knows cheap doesn't work.
- Tried expensive and it failed → understand *why* so you don't reproduce the failure.

## After the four

You either say:

> Here's what I think would work for you, and here's what it costs.

Or you say:

> Honestly, I don't think I'm the right fit for what you need right now. Here's why.

Both are useful. Both build credibility. The people you tell no remember you and refer.

## Brand reasoning

Each question is structured to enact the brand foundations (see `brand-foundations.md`):

- **Question 1** treats the contractor as the authority on his own business. The brand never educates the customer about his own world.
- **Question 2** respects the trigger as real. The brand exists because external pressure made training necessary — not because training is good in some abstract sense.
- **Question 3** is the load-bearing one. It enacts the deeper frame: the contractor owns the knowledge; the job is to transmit it. Asking *what does a new guy need to know* puts him in the role of master and you in the role of scribe.
- **Question 4** assumes he's tried things and respects that. Doesn't condescend. Doesn't pretend you're the first idea he's had.

The shape of the call mirrors the shape of the product: he provides the knowledge, you provide the transmission craft.

## What not to ask

- "What are your training needs?" — jargon. Vendor language. Triggers the shutdown.
- "Are you OSHA compliant?" — implies you're qualified to judge. You're not. That's his CSP's lane.
- "How big is your safety budget?" — too direct, too early. The trigger question gets you there indirectly.
- "Have you heard of [competitor]?" — never put competitor names in his head. If he raises them, fine. Otherwise don't.
